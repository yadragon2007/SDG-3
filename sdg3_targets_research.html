SDG-3 targets fully achieved as of 2025

Ten years into the 2030 Agenda, none of the 13 SDG-3 targets have been fully achieved globally. COVID-19 severely set back progress in maternal health, TB, immunization, and universal health coverage. Egypt shows impressive gains in several targets but faces critical burdens from NCDs and road traffic injuries. The Middle East is highly heterogeneous — Gulf states approach First World metrics while conflict-affected countries (Yemen, Syria) have regressed. First World nations lead but face rising NCD and mental health challenges.

Egypt spotlight: Egypt's national Hepatitis C elimination program — screening over 62 million people and treating over 4 million — is one of the most successful public health campaigns in MENA history. Egypt has gone from the highest HCV burden in the world to a model WHO cites globally for elimination strategy.

Egypt NCD crisis: NCDs cause 82% of all deaths in Egypt and 67% of premature deaths. Risk factors are alarming: 40% hypertension prevalence, 17% diabetes rate, and some of the world's highest obesity rates. Out-of-pocket health spending is ~60% of total health expenditure, creating catastrophic financial burdens for many Egyptians.

Globally, only 1 in 11 people with substance use disorders received treatment in 2022 — and the rate is DECLINING (from 11% in 2015 to under 9% in 2022). This is the only SDG-3 target where coverage is moving in the wrong direction globally. In Egypt and the Middle East, stigma and limited resources create near-total treatment gaps.

Egypt: Road traffic injuries are among the most problematic SDG-3 areas for Egypt and across North Africa. Infrastructure challenges, enforcement gaps, and rapid motorization drive high fatality rates. All six North African countries studied are lagging on this target for 2030.

Egypt has made strong gains in antenatal care (from 50% to 89% coverage between 1990 and 2014) and skilled birth attendance (~92%). However, adolescent birth rates remain a concern at ~55/1,000 — roughly 4× higher than First World averages. The global adolescent birth rate fell from 66 to 38 per 1,000 between 2000 and 2024.

Egypt's UHC reform: Egypt enacted the Universal Health Insurance Law in 2018, rolling out coverage in phases starting with Port Said governorate. Progress is real but gradual — index ~67 vs global 71. The key challenge: out-of-pocket payments constitute ~60% of total health spending, pushing millions into financial hardship. Global SCI reached 71/100 in 2023 but is projected to only hit 74 by 2030 — short of universal coverage. 4.6 billion people remain without full essential health coverage.

Egypt has a 24% overall smoking prevalence and growing shisha use. WHO has conducted 3 key tobacco control studies in Egypt. The government has been urged to raise tobacco taxes to the WHO-recommended 75% of retail price — current levels (~40%) are insufficient as a deterrent. Waterpipe tobacco presents a particular challenge given cultural norms across MENA.

Egypt is the largest pharmaceutical manufacturer in the MENA region and played a key role in COVID-19 vaccine production for Africa through its partnership with Sinovac and AstraZeneca. Childhood vaccination rates (~94% DTP3) are strong. However, 68 million children globally were un- or under-vaccinated as of 2022 — largely in low-income countries — a COVID-era regression.

Egypt faces a significant health workforce deficit — 9 physicians per 10,000 population against a WHO minimum of 23. Brain drain to Gulf countries and Europe is a persistent challenge. The new Universal Health Insurance system aims to improve health financing, but the burden of out-of-pocket spending at 60% remains catastrophically high for low-income Egyptians.

In May 2025, countries adopted the first-ever global pandemic preparedness agreement — a landmark achievement for SDG 3.d. Egypt and MENA countries showed moderate preparedness during COVID-19. The US withdrawal from WHO in 2025 has complicated global health security financing, with particular impacts on African and MENA programs that depend on US-funded WHO operations.
