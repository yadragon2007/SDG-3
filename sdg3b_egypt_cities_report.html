SDG Target Definition
What Does SDG 3.b Require?

SDG Target 3.b calls on countries to: (1) support research and development of vaccines and medicines for communicable and non-communicable diseases; (2) provide access to affordable essential medicines and vaccines, particularly in developing countries; and (3) implement the TRIPS Agreement flexibilities to allow use of intellectual property flexibilities for public health. It is measured by: proportion of the population with access to affordable essential medicines on a sustainable basis; proportion of the target population covered by vaccines included in national immunization programmes; and total net official development assistance (ODA) for medical research and basic health.

3.b Overview: Egypt is the largest pharmaceutical manufacturer in MENA, producing ~95% of its medicine needs domestically through ~130 companies. Yet significant supply-chain failures, geographic inequality in distribution, and persistent vaccine cold-chain gaps mean that millions in rural Upper Egypt and border governorates lack reliable access to essential medicines and routine vaccinations. The national 94% DTP3 average conceals governorate-level rates as low as 78–82% in the most affected areas.

Key tension: Egypt scores well nationally on DTP3 vaccination (94%) — above the global average of 84% — yet performs poorly on COVID-19 coverage (~45%) and essential medicine availability (~65% in public sector vs. 95%+ in First World nations). The national averages mask profound governorate-level inequalities explored in this report.

⚠ Cold-chain crisis: Egypt's national vaccination programme depends on a cold-chain infrastructure concentrated in urban centres. In 20 of 27 governorates, at least one district health office has reported cold-chain equipment failures or stock-outs in the past 3 years (WHO EMRO data). Upper Egypt and border governorates are most affected. An estimated 1.8–2.4 million children under-5 in these areas have incomplete or delayed vaccination schedules — a direct 3.b gap.

The structural divide: All 4 Critical governorates are in Upper Egypt, where DTP3 coverage falls to 78–80% (vs. 97% in Cairo) and essential medicine availability in public facilities is only 38–43% (vs. 80% in Cairo). This 40-point gap in the composite 3.b index between Sohag City and Cairo is driven by a convergence of supply-chain failures, cold-chain infrastructure deficits, low COVID-19 vaccination uptake, and near-total dependence on out-of-pocket pharmaceutical spending. These findings align with the NCD Map Egypt document and WHO EMRO reports in the project archive.

Recommendations
Priority Interventions for SDG 3.b in Egypt

Drawing from WHO best-practice guidance, MENA regional health frameworks, and the project's reference documents (NCD Map Egypt, WHO UHC Report, EPRS Briefing), the following interventions are ranked by urgency.

Immediate (0–2 yrs)
Emergency Access
  • Upgrade cold-chain in all Upper Egypt district stores
  • Deploy mobile vaccine units to Sohag, Qena, Luxor, Assiut
  • Emergency essential medicine stockpile for 10 critical gov.
  • Community health worker programme for vaccine outreach
  • Free childhood vaccine campaigns targeting rural areas
Short-Term (2–4 yrs)
Supply Chain Reform
  • Decentralise pharmaceutical distribution to governorate level
  • Expand generic medicine production by domestic manufacturers
  • Establish medicine access subsidy for below-poverty households
  • Complete COVID-19 booster campaign in all border gov.
  • Enforce TRIPS flexibilities for NCD essential medicines
Medium-Term (4–7 yrs)
R&D & System
  • Raise health R&D spending from 0.3% to 0.8% of GDP
  • Establish regional pharmaceutical distribution hubs in Assiut & Sohag
  • Join African Medicines Agency (AMA) regulatory framework
  • Develop national digital medicine inventory system
  • Achieve 95%+ DTP3 coverage in all 27 governorates by 2028
