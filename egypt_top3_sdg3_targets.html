Root cause analysis: NCDs in Egypt are fuelled by a convergence of crisis-level risk factors. The STEPwise WHO survey found 66% of Egyptian women are overweight, 42% obese, and 24% smoke — combined with nearly 75% of adults not performing vigorous physical activity. Cancer rates rose from 127 to 166 per 100,000 between 2007 and 2022. Only ~7% of Egypt's GDP is spent on all healthcare, making NCD prevention and management severely underfunded.

Priority Actions for Egypt
1Scale the WHO-Egypt NCD pilot (primary care integration) from 17 to 500+ centers nationally by 2027, targeting hypertension and diabetes screening for all adults over 40.
2Implement mandatory front-of-pack nutrition labelling and sugar/salt reduction targets in processed food — Egypt has no binding food reformulation policy as of 2025.
3Develop and fund a National Cancer Control Plan — Egypt has been working on this with WHO but implementation remains partial as of 2024.
4Expand the national diabetes registry and link it to the new UHIS system for coordinated chronic disease management pathways.

The UHI Law is Egypt's most important health reform — enacted in 2018, it mandates coverage for all citizens through a social health insurance model. Phase 1 launched in Port Said with World Bank support ($3B loan). However, at the current pace, full national rollout is not expected until the early 2030s. Meanwhile, with government health spending falling to just 1.2% of GDP in 2024–25 against a constitutional obligation of 3%, Egypt's health system operates on one of the most severe funding deficits in its modern history.

Priority Actions for Egypt
1Accelerate UHIS Phase 2 and 3 rollout — covering the 8 Phase 1 governorates fully and beginning the next 8 by 2026, reaching 50% national coverage by 2028.
2Enforce the constitutional mandate: government health spending must reach 3% of GDP minimum. The current 1.2% rate is legally unconstitutional.
3Build 20,300 primary healthcare facilities by 2035 as projected by UNICEF — at least 2,000–3,000 prioritised in rural Upper Egypt and underserved Delta areas by 2027.
4Introduce OOP caps and co-payment exemptions for the bottom 40% income bracket as part of UHIS benefits package refinement.

The 2024 data shows a first encouraging signal: fatalities fell 10.2% from 5,861 in 2023 to 5,260 in 2024 — Egypt's government has invested ~EGP 3 trillion in roads over the past decade, adding 144,300 km of paved roads. However, injuries rose 7.5% in the same year, suggesting road expansion is creating new high-speed corridors that increase injury risk even as some safety measures take effect. Enforcement gaps, the absence of speed camera coverage outside major highways, and no mandatory BAC (blood-alcohol) testing remain key structural gaps.

Priority Actions for Egypt
1Deploy speed camera networks across the 6 highest-mortality governorates (Cairo, Behira, Alexandria, Assiut, North Sinai, Matruh) — currently enforcement is concentrated on the desert highways.
2Mandate helmet use for all motorcyclists nationally with police enforcement — Egypt's motorcycle fatality rate per 10,000 vehicles is one of MENA's highest.
3Launch a national road safety data system integrating MoH death certificates, Ministry of Interior crash reports, and hospital injury data — current under-reporting is estimated at 30–40% by WHO.
4Target the 15–24 male cohort with road safety education campaigns — this group accounts for 23.2% of all road deaths despite comprising ~15% of the population.
